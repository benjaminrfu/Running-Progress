DATA 303 Final Project

Tracking Running Progress Through Strava Data

An analysis of pace, heart rate, and training consistency from 2023–2026.

Benjamin Fu and Athena Liao

Research Question

How has Ben’s running fitness changed from 2023 to 2026, and what patterns in pace, heart rate, and training volume explain that progress?

1. Pace

Has average running pace improved over time?

2. Efficiency

Has heart rate decreased at similar pace ranges?

3. Consistency

Do changes in training volume help explain periods of improvement?

Data and Cleaning

This project uses Ben’s personal Strava export, which includes 588 activities recorded between 2023 and 2026. The original file contained many variables that were either empty or not relevant to the final analysis, so the data was cleaned before visualization.

The cleaning process included filtering to running activities, removing columns with large amounts of missing data, converting dates into usable time variables, converting distance into miles, and creating a pace variable measured in minutes per mile. These steps made the data easier to interpret from a runner’s perspective.

Dataset Summary

  • Source: Personal Strava export
  • Time period: 2023–2026
  • Original size: 588 activities
  • Main variables: distance, date, pace, heart rate, year, month
  • Goal: evaluate running progress over time

Key Takeaways

1. Pace alone does not tell the full story.

Pace is useful, but it can be affected by distance, workout type, weather, terrain, and training purpose. Looking at pace alongside heart rate gives a more complete picture of running progress.

2. Heart rate at similar paces suggests improved efficiency.

If the final chart continues to show lower heart rate at similar paces in later years, that would suggest Ben became more efficient as a runner over time.

3. Training consistency helps explain improvement.

Monthly mileage can show whether stronger running periods were connected to more consistent training. This helps move the analysis beyond describing progress and toward explaining why it may have happened.

Conclusion

Overall, this project uses personal running data to evaluate improvement from multiple angles. The combination of pace trends, heart rate comparisons, and training volume provides a clearer picture than any single metric alone. The final results suggest that running progress should be measured not only by faster times, but also by improved efficiency and consistent training habits.
